Section 1 · Fundamentals

What is Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE) and why does it matter for carbon?

PUE is one of the most important public metrics for data center energy efficiency. It tells you how much of a facility's electricity actually goes to computing and how much is lost to overhead. The majority of that overhead goes to cooling, and that overhead has a direct, calculable carbon cost driven by three things: climate, the cleanliness of the energy supply, and government carbon policy.

Step 1 · Overhead MW
Cooling draws power equal to (PUE − 1) × IT load. At PUE 1.09, a 100MW facility wastes 9MW on overhead — 24/7, 365 days a year, before a single computation runs.
Step 2 · Annual kWh
9MW × 8,760 hrs = 78.8M kWh per year lost to overhead. That's the electricity bill for keeping servers running before accounting for useful work.
Step 3 · Grid intensity
Each kWh carries a carbon cost based on how electricity is generated. Finland's grid: 57 gCO₂/kWh (nuclear + hydro). Taiwan's grid: 635 gCO₂/kWh (coal + LNG).
Step 4 · The gap
CO₂ tons × local carbon price = what they actually pay. CO₂ tons × EU ETS ($70.37/ton) = what they should pay. The difference is the hidden subsidy baked into every build decision.

Google is the only major hyperscaler that publishes quarterly, facility-level PUE data — 30 facilities, updated every quarter. In 2024, Google reported a global average PUE of 1.09, representing 40% less cooling overhead than AWS (1.15) and 44% less than Microsoft (1.16). AWS reports a single global average. Microsoft reports regionally. That transparency gap matters.

Using Google's 2025 data across 10 selected facilities, and a consistent assumption of a 100MW IT load hyperscale campus, this analysis estimates three things for each location: the annual cost of cooling electricity, the CO₂ produced by that cooling, and the carbon cost gap — how much more each facility would owe if carbon were priced at the EU ETS standard of $70.37 per ton versus what the local government currently charges. What the numbers show is that the gap is real, large, and almost entirely explained by two things: how dirty the local grid is, and how close to zero the local carbon price is. The two cheapest places to build — Riyadh and Jakarta — also carry the largest unpriced carbon bills. Low electricity cost doesn't mean clean infrastructure. It means the true cost is being absorbed somewhere else.

Three policy asks
Ask 1
Require data centers to publish facility-level PUE and carbon disclosure.
Require data centers to publish facility-level PUE and carbon disclosure as a condition of operating. This entire analysis exists because Google publishes quarterly PUE data for every facility. Governments approving large data center builds — especially in Southeast Asia and the Middle East — should make this a requirement. You can't hold companies accountable for what they don't have to report.
Ask 2
Price carbon where it actually matters.
The EU Emissions Trading System works because it makes emissions show up in the cost of electricity. Power plants pay. That cost flows through. Large users like data centers feel it. The problem is that most fast-growing data center markets don't have anything similar — which means companies can build in carbon-heavy regions without paying for the emissions that come with it.
Ask 3
Extend CBAM principles to digital infrastructure.
The Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism already does this for physical goods. If steel is produced in a low-carbon-price country and sold in Europe, the importer pays the difference. That same logic applies to compute. Right now, a data center in a low-carbon-price region can serve users in high-carbon-price markets with no adjustment. Extending CBAM to digital services is complex, but even partial adoption would push companies to disclose more, build more efficiently, and think twice about treating low-carbon-price regions as a hidden subsidy.

Section 4 · Carbon Policy Gap

What is the gap between what facilities pay today vs. what EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) would charge?

Since the EU Emissions Trading System is the most widely used benchmark for a credible carbon price, we use the current market price of $70.37 per ton of CO₂ as our benchmark — though that number is still below the true social cost of carbon, which economists estimate at $185 to $283 per ton. X axis: annual CO₂ from cooling. Y axis: carbon cost gap vs EU ETS. Positive = underpaying. Negative = overpaying (carbon policy working). Bubble size = annual cooling electricity cost, which shows the financial scale of each facility's cooling operation — a larger bubble means a higher energy spend, letting you see which facilities face the largest carbon gaps in both relative and absolute dollar terms. US facilities shown as diamonds — no national electricity pricing available.

What explains PUE variation? (N=426 quarterly observations, 30 facilities)
~25% climate
~75% engineering + operations

Climate explains ~25% of PUE variation across Google's fleet. The remaining 75% belongs to the companies: cooling system design, hardware generation, AI-assisted energy optimization, facility age, and operational discipline. The model prediction error is ±0.030 PUE units — enough to benchmark a location, not precise enough to engineer against.

Section 6 · Growing Markets

What PUE should a hyperscaler target in regions with growing data center demand?

The markets analyzed here represent the next wave of hyperscale investment: Seoul, Tokyo, Taipei, and Singapore in Asia-Pacific; Mumbai, Jakarta, Kuala Lumpur, and Bangkok in South and Southeast Asia; and Riyadh and Dubai in the Middle East. These markets are growing for three intersecting reasons: government-led digital economy initiatives, rapid AI adoption by local enterprises, and hyperscaler expansion driven by low electricity costs. That last driver is also what makes the carbon story important — low electricity cost often signals fossil-heavy generation, not efficiency.
