Section 1 · Model 1 — Weather → Vintage Score

Can weather alone predict whether a Bordeaux vintage will be great?

Bordeaux wines are released as en primeur futures — buyers pay 100% upfront for unfinished barrel wine, 18–24 months before delivery, based on early critic barrel sample scores. The price is set after critics taste. The score is set by weather. Princeton economist Orley Ashenfelter proved in 1988 that three weather variables predict Bordeaux vintage quality better than any expert. Parker called him a "total sham." History proved Ashenfelter right.

Variable 1 · Avg Temp (Apr–Sep)
+1.85 pts per +1°C growing season temperature. Warmer summers concentrate sugars and phenolics — the dominant positive driver in the model.
Variable 2 · Harvest Rain (Aug–Sep)
−1.19 pts per +10mm of harvest rainfall. Rain dilutes fruit and risks botrytis rot — the single biggest negative driver.
Variable 3 · Winter Rain (Oct–Mar)
+0.83 pts per +10mm of winter rainfall. Deep soil water reserves that vines draw on through the growing season — positive but secondary.
2025 Forecast
18.9°C growing season · 22mm harvest rain (2nd driest in 40 years). Model predicts 95.9 pts (95% CI: 89–102). Critics are saying 97–100.

The CV R² gap (72.5% in-sample vs 48.8% out-of-sample) is meaningful but expected. Weather predicts vintage quality directionally but not precisely. The model is best understood as a signal, not an oracle. Extreme years — the 1991 spring frost that destroyed 40% of buds, the 2003 European heatwave that ran 5°C above normal — fall outside what a linear weather model can capture.

The 2025 gap tells its own story. The model predicts 95.9 pts. Critics are saying 97–100 for top châteaux. That 2–4pt gap between model and critic is where prestige and hype live. Nature sets the ceiling. Humans decide how close to reach it — and what story to tell on the way up.

Section 2 · Model 1 — Coefficients & Fit

How accurately does the model predict vintage scores — and where does it fail?

The regression isolates three independent signals. All three are statistically significant at p<0.001. Growing season temperature is the dominant positive driver. Harvest rainfall is the biggest drag. Winter rainfall provides the deep-soil water reserve vines draw on through summer.

The model fails in two systematic ways. First, extreme weather events fall outside the range of normal variation it was trained on — 1991's spring frost that eliminated 40% of buds shows up as a massive underestimate; 2003's European heatwave pushed temperatures so far beyond historical norms that the linear model loses its grip. Second, the model predicts one score for the entire Left Bank, ignoring appellation-level microclimate variation between Pauillac, St-Julien, Margaux, St-Estèphe, and Pessac-Léognan.

Ashenfelter's insight: The wine establishment mocked a Princeton economist for saying weather predicts wine quality better than expert palates. He published anyway. Twenty years of results proved him right. His framework is now used in climate research to project how rising temperatures will shift Bordeaux vintage quality by 2050.

Section 3 · The 1855 Classification

Does a 170-year-old classification system still predict château quality today?

Weather can predict average Left Bank vintage quality, but each château has its own terroir, winemaking philosophy, and accumulated reputation — none of which weather captures. The closest measurable proxy from public data is the 1855 Classification — a list Napoleon III's government created to showcase French wines at the Universal Exhibition in Paris. The brokers didn't taste anything. They looked at which châteaux commanded the highest prices over the previous decades.

I'll be honest — I was skeptical. Using a ranking created in 1855 by brokers who never tasted the wine feels like a joke. But when I added it to the model, R² jumped from 29.8% to 68.2%. It's outdated, politically frozen, and widely criticised by wine experts. And it still works. The most likely reason: prestige compounds over 170 years. Being a First Growth in 1855 meant higher prices, more investment, better winemaking — which made you a better First Growth in 1900, and 1950, and today. The classification doesn't measure quality. It measures accumulated advantage.

Section 5 · Over & Underperformers

Which châteaux consistently outperform — and underperform — their classification?

Model 2 predicts what a château should score given the vintage quality and its 1855 tier. The residual — actual minus predicted — tells you which châteaux consistently beat expectations and which consistently disappoint. These are multi-year averages across 2000–2024, so they reflect genuine structural differences, not single-vintage noise.

The stories behind the numbers
Overperformer ★
Palmer +1.68 pts
A Third Growth that performs like a First. Classified in 1855, Palmer suffered from lack of investment at the time — critics believe it would have ranked Second Growth had its potential been recognised. Since Thomas Duroux became winemaker in 2004, it has scored on par with Château Margaux. The +1.68 residual persists across 25 years. It's not noise.
Overperformer ★
Pontet-Canet +1.50 pts
The first fully certified biodynamic château in the Médoc. After transitioning in 2010, Pontet-Canet went from reliable Fifth Growth to occasionally matching the First Growths. Parker gave the 2010 vintage 100 points. The market now prices it like a Second Growth despite its Fifth Growth classification.
Overperformer ★
Lynch-Bages +1.10 pts
A Fifth Growth that has quietly scored like a Third for two decades. No biodynamic certification, no obvious structural explanation — just consistently excellent winemaking under the Cazes family. The classification hasn't caught up with the quality.

The underperformers: d'Armailhac (−1.62 pts ★) and Lagrange (−1.32 pts ★) both consistently underperform their classification peers. Less critical attention, less brand investment, and the scores reflect it. These are multi-year averages — the signal is structural, not noise.

Section 6 · Biodynamic Farming

Does biodynamic farming produce measurably better scores and higher prices?

Biodynamic farming treats the vineyard as a self-contained ecosystem — no synthetic pesticides or fertilisers, composting, cover crops, and farming timed to lunar cycles. It goes further than organic. We added a biodynamic dummy to Model 3 (the price model) — coded 1 for Pontet-Canet from 2010 and Palmer from 2017, when each received formal certification.

The key finding: Once critics have scored the wine, the 1855 Classification adds no statistically significant price power. Score explains everything. The ranking that determines how collectors think about these wines — the tier that shapes which bottles end up in investment portfolios — disappears when you actually look at the data. Classification matters only because it shapes what critics expect to taste, and what buyers expect to pay. The moment the score is published, the tier is irrelevant.

Section 8 · Price vs Quality

Which châteaux are mispriced relative to their quality?

The model predicts price from score, vintage quality, classification, and biodynamic status. Châteaux where actual price far exceeds predicted price carry a prestige premium the model can't explain. Châteaux where actual price falls below predicted are potentially undervalued relative to what the score would justify.

Underpriced relative to quality
Montrose · 2nd Growth value
In 2022, Montrose scored 98pts — matching the top First Growths — and sold for $233. Model predicts ~$341. Montrose (St-Estèphe, 2nd Growth) carries less brand recognition than Pauillac peers at the same quality level. The data suggests it may be structurally underpriced relative to what the score would justify.
The closing line
The gap is the story
The ~25% MAE in Model 3 isn't a model failure — it's a feature. The part of price the model can't explain is the prestige premium. Palmer's $306 gap is that premium in dollar terms. Nature makes the wine. Humans make the price. The gap between the two is where all the money lives.
